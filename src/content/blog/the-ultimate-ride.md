---
title: "The Ultimate Ride"
description: "On parenthood, life's theme park, and the different lines we choose to stand in."
date: 2026-05-25
tags: ["life", "parenting"]
---

Ten years is a long time to go without seeing a friend, but having young children is a fast equalizer. Recently, I reconnected with an old friend who just moved to the Bay Area from China with his wife and two kids. Since we also have two kids around the exact same age, the shared exhaustion and shared joy were immediately understood.

We were joined by a few mutual friends—recently married, but without kids. Sitting in the same room, the contrast in how we seek out life's meaning was striking.

For our child-free friends, meaning often comes in deliberate bursts of adrenaline: bungee jumping, extreme sports, chasing the next thrill. For young parents, especially those of us navigating it without extended family nearby to help shoulder the load, the reality is fundamentally different. It isn't a fleeting, life-or-death extreme. It's more of a "slow pain." It's the daily, unrelenting grind and the harshness of sacrificing your own baseline quality of life to keep tiny humans thriving.

But there is a reason our genes are programmed for this.

If life is a theme park, having kids is the ultimate attraction. The biological reward—that profound, subconscious, genetic happiness—is the highest peak in the park. Yes, choosing to be a parent is exactly like standing in a grueling, three-hour line just to get strapped into the scariest roller coaster on the map. You *can* choose to walk past it. You can skip the wait, avoid the terror, and preserve your free time for other attractions. But if you do, you're missing out on the best ride in the entire park.

Ultimately, how you spend your day at the park is up to you. Some people choose to get back in line for this ride multiple times, spending half their visit right there, happily foregoing other equally incredible attractions. Others make a conscious, deliberate choice early on to skip it, dedicating their time to experiencing every other meaningful ride the park has to offer, fully aware of the trade-offs they are making. Both are perfectly valid ways to use your ticket.

Life's timing adds its own twists to the park. There are those who finally decide they want to brave the coaster, only to realize the park closes in three hours—too late to even get in line, leaving them to find their joy and meaning elsewhere. There are people who have been sweating in line for an hour and realize it's too hard to back out now. And then there are those currently ratcheting up the steepest, scariest part of the track, terrified and questioning their choices, completely unaware that the greatest thrill and payoff of their life is right around the corner.

Wherever you find yourself, there is no right or wrong choice. There is a reason a theme park has so many different attractions to offer. The only question is: which line are you standing in, and are you making the most of the ride?
